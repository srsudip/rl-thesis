Slide 2 · The Problem

Why Kenya Needs This System

Kenya is rolling out the Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC). At Grade 9, every student must be placed into one of three Senior Secondary pathways. Get this wrong and the student spends 3 years in the wrong direction — affecting university choice, career, and life trajectory.

The Stakes

  • !
    ~1.2 million students per cohort transitioning to Senior Secondary annually.
  • !
    3 pathways · 12 tracks — wrong placement is hard to reverse.
  • ·
    Teachers often rely on raw KJSEA scores alone — ignoring 5 years of trajectory data (G4–G9).
  • ·
    Subjective bias creeps in: gender stereotypes, school resources, family pressure.
  • ·
    No existing tool considers student preferences alongside aptitude.

A Real Misplacement Risk

A student scoring 72% average but with Mathematics at 18% would be flagged "above average" by simple ranking — yet placing them in STEM would be catastrophic. Math is non-negotiable for STEM. Our system enforces this as a hard gate.

What We're Building

A two-layer intelligent system that combines hard rules (KNEC-aligned) with adaptive learning (RL).

Layer 1 — Deterministic Recommender

Computes the formal Cluster Weight per pathway using official KICD/KNEC subject weights. Applies hard core-subject gates. Provides the "what".

Layer 2 — Reinforcement Learning Coach

A Deep Q-Network learns from student trajectories which actions (improvement recommendations) historically led to better outcomes. Provides the "how" — what to work on next.

Project Oath

"We are playing god with a student's future. Every recommendation must be defensible by curriculum policy, audited by a human, and overrideable by a teacher."

Layer 1 — Deterministic Recommender

The "judge". Computes which pathway formally fits the student per KICD policy. Uses three quantitative signals.

  • 1
    Cluster Weight (60%) — official KNEC weighted average across subjects.
  • 2
    Cosine Similarity (25%) — vector angle between student's score profile and ideal pathway profile.
  • 3
    PSI (15%) — Pathway Suitability Index from competency profile.

Then enforces hard gates: core subjects must score ≥ AE1 (31%). Falls back through 3 tiers if no pathway is fully eligible.

Layer 2 — RL Coaching Agent

The "tutor". Looks at the student's trajectory and recommends what to focus on next to maximise long-term suitability.

  • a
    Observes the 78-dim state (current scores, history, deltas, suitability, preference).
  • b
    Decides 1 of 9 discrete actions (e.g. "strengthen math", "explore SS over STEM", "deepen current").
  • c
    Learns from real student trajectories: which actions historically improved suitability in the next grade?

Both layers feed into a single human-readable result slip. A teacher can override anything.

Top-Level Design Principles

No Database

All data lives as CSV files. Generated in-memory then persisted. Zero infrastructure overhead — runs anywhere Python runs.

Reproducible Everything

Every component accepts a seed. Same seed → identical outputs. Critical for thesis defence and audit trails.

Vectorised Throughout

No Python loops in the hot path. NumPy broadcasting + matrix multiply. 500 students generate in < 0.2 s — a 430× speedup over the original looped version.

Decoupled Layers

src/data and src/rl are independent packages. pages/dashboard.py is the only consumer of both. No circular imports.

Test-First Where It Matters

91 integration tests using real generated data — never mocks. Catches edge-case grade distributions that unit mocks would miss.

Item Response Theory (IRT) — The Foundation of Realistic Data

Kenya's KJSEA dataset is too small (and too sensitive) for safe RL training. We generate synthetic students that statistically mimic real ones, using IRT — the same math behind SAT, GRE, PISA, TOEFL.

What is IRT, in 60 seconds?

Imagine you give 1000 students 50 math questions. You notice three things:

  • Some students consistently answer more questions correctly — they have higher ability.
  • Some questions are harder — fewer students get them right at any ability level.
  • Some questions are better at separating strong from weak students (high discrimination); others are answered correctly by everyone or no one.

IRT models all three at once. The probability that student i answers question j correctly depends on the student's latent ability θ minus the question's difficulty b, scaled by how much the question discriminates a.

Latent variable θ ("theta") Think of θ as the student's hidden talent level — you can't measure it directly, but you can infer it from their answers. Each subject has its own θ. A student can have high θ in maths and low θ in arts.

Why not use random scores?

Random scores have no internal structure. A student strong in Maths in G5 might be weak in Maths in G6. The DQN cannot learn anything from such noise. IRT gives us:

  • Temporal consistency — students grow over time
  • Cross-subject correlation — strong in INT_SCI ⟹ likely strong in MATH
  • Realistic difficulty spread — harder subjects produce lower averages

The Three Core Equations

θi,k,g = αi,k + βi,k · (g − g0)

Latent ability of student i in competency k at grade g

P(correct) = σ(a · θ − b)

Logistic function maps ability to probability. σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e−x)

score = 100 · P(correct) + ε

ε ~ Normal(0, σ=5) · Gaussian measurement noise

Parameter Decoder

bDifficulty of questionNormal(μsubj, 0.5)

Slide 6 · IRT Worked Example

From Latent Ability to a Realistic Score — Walking Through One Student

Let's generate a single Mathematics score for a synthetic Grade 6 student named "Wanjiku". We'll trace every parameter through the three equations.

Step 1 · Sample Wanjiku's Latent Parameters

From the multivariate normal distribution we draw her starting ability (α) and growth rate (β) for the MATH competency:

αWanjiku, MATH = +0.6 (slightly above-avg starter)
βWanjiku, MATH = +0.25 (steady positive growth per grade)

Step 2 · Compute θ at Grade 6

θ = 0.6 + 0.25 × (6 − 4) = 1.10

Her "true" math ability at Grade 6, on the standardized θ scale (where 0 = average, 1 = roughly 1 σ above average).

Step 3 · Pick a Question

We pick one Mathematics indicator (a fine-grained skill) from the curriculum. Its IRT parameters were sampled earlier:

a = 1.2 (good discrimination)
b = 0.30 (slightly hard — Math has +0.3 difficulty offset)

Step 4 · Probability of Correct

η = a·θ − b = 1.2 × 1.10 − 0.30 = 1.02
P = 1 / (1 + e−1.02) = 1 / (1 + 0.360) = 0.735

Wanjiku has a 73.5% chance of answering this question correctly.

Step 5 · Convert to a Score

score = 100 × 0.735 + ε, ε ~ N(0, 5)
score = 73.5 + (−1.8) = 71.7

After noise injection, her score on this single Mathematics indicator is 71.7 — corresponding to ME1 (Meeting Expectations) on the CBC scale.

The Aggregation

This entire calculation runs for every (student × indicator) pair. There are ~600 indicators per grade across all subjects, and we generate 1000+ students. Final subject scores are the average of all indicator scores within that subject. Wanjiku's aggregate Math score might end up at ~70%, her INT_SCI at ~65%, her CRE_ARTS at ~52% — all internally consistent with her competency profile.

Why this matters for the DQN

Because Wanjiku's scores across subjects are derived from the same latent θ vector, the DQN can learn meaningful patterns: "if a student improved in MATH between G7 and G8, did their INT_SCI also improve?" Yes — with high probability, because the underlying competencies are correlated. Random data would destroy this signal.

Slide 7 · Vectorised Generation Pipeline

How Thousands of Students Are Generated in Seconds

The naive approach (a Python loop for each student × subject × indicator × grade) would take hours. Our pipeline is fully vectorised numpy — the entire 1000-student G4–G9 dataset generates in under 2 seconds.

The 6-Step Pipeline

  • 1
    Load curriculum CSVs — KICD-defined hierarchy: Subject → Strand → Substrand → Indicator. Each indicator maps to one or more competencies.
  • 2
    Sample (α, β) per student per competency from multivariate normal distribution. Shape: (n_students, 2 × n_competencies).
  • 3
    Build θ tensor for all (student × grade × competency) combinations: (n_stu, n_grades, n_comp). Computed in one line of vectorised numpy.
  • 4
    Sample indicator IRT params — per-indicator (a, b) parameters. Discrimination from log-normal, difficulty from subject-conditional normal.
  • 5
    Compute response matrix in one matrix multiplication: η = Θ @ A.T − b → logistic → score + noise.
  • 6
    Aggregate to subject level — mean of indicator scores per (student, grade, subject). Saves to generated_*.csv.

The Covariance Structure (Why It's Realistic)

Real students aren't independent across competencies. A student strong in Logical Reasoning tends to also be strong in Numerical Fluency. We capture this with a structured covariance matrix:

Σ = [ Σα Σαβ ]
    [ ΣαβT Σβ ]

Σα(i,j) = σα2 · ρα (i ≠ j)
Σβ(i,j) = σβ2 · ρβ (i ≠ j)
Σαβ(i,i) = ραβ · σασβ

With our chosen values:

ParamValueEffect
σα0.7Spread of starting abilities
σβ0.35Spread of growth rates
ρα+0.3Cross-competency correlation
ρβ+0.3Growth correlation
ραβ−0.2Mean reversion: high starters grow slower

The −0.2 detail

Negative α-β correlation models a real phenomenon: students who start very high have less room to grow (ceiling effect). Without this, top students would diverge to unrealistic scores by Grade 9.

Slide 8 · Signal 1 — Cluster Weight

Cluster Weight Calculation — The KNEC Formula, Step by Step

The Cluster Weight (CW) is the primary KNEC-aligned signal. For each pathway, it answers: "How well does this student's weighted score profile match what this pathway requires?" We follow the official 2025 KICD/KNEC methodology exactly.

Step 1 · Blend Across Time

We don't only use Grade 9 scores — that would let a student game the system with last-minute coaching. KNEC blends three time periods:

blended = 0.60×KJSEA + 0.20×SBA + 0.20×KPSEA
SourcePeriodWeight
KJSEAGrade 9 final exam60%
SBASchool-based assessment, G7–G820%
KPSEAGrade 6 primary exit exam20%

Step 2 · Z-Score Normalisation

Raw scores can't be compared across subjects directly — Maths is harder than PE, so a 60 in Maths is more impressive than a 60 in PE. We normalise against the population mean and standard deviation:

z = 50 + 10 × (score − μpop) / σpop

A student scoring 65 in Math (where μ=50, σ=15) gets z = 50 + 10×(65−50)/15 = 60.0. Above average. Same student scoring 65 in PE (where μ=70, σ=10) gets z = 50 + 10×(65−70)/10 = 45.0. Below average.

Step 3 · Weighted Average per Pathway

Each pathway has different weights for each subject — see table below. For each pathway:

CW(pathway) = Σ(zi × wi) / Σ(wi)

Step 4 · Trajectory Stability Bonus

final_CW = 0.85 × current + 0.15 × prev × stability

Rewards students who are consistently strong in a pathway over time, vs. students who jumped up at the last grade.

Slide 10 · The Three Signals Combined

The Composite Score — Three Independent Signals

CW alone could be gamed or could mislead. We combine it with two complementary signals that measure different things. The final ranking comes from a weighted sum.

composite = 0.60 × CW + 0.25 × CosineSim + 0.15 × PSI

Signal 1 · Cluster Weight

Weight: 60%

The official KNEC quantitative measure. Z-scored, weighted, blended over time.

Measures: Formal eligibility per policy.

CW = Σ(zi·wi) / Σwi

Signal 2 · Cosine Similarity

Weight: 25%

Treats the student's score profile as a vector. Treats the "ideal" pathway profile as another vector. Measures the angle between them.

Measures: Profile shape match — independent of magnitude.

cos(s, p) = s·p / (‖s‖ · ‖p‖)

A student with [80,60,70] in STEM subjects has a different shape than [70,70,70] — even if averages match.

Signal 3 · PSI

Weight: 15%

Pathway Suitability Index — derived from the underlying competency profile (the 6 KICD competencies that survived from IRT).

Measures: Latent ability fit, beneath subject scores.

PSI = Σ(competencyk·affinityk)
Why three signals instead of one?

Each signal can fail in a different way. CW can be gamed by inflating one high-weight subject. Cosine can match shape but miss absolute level. PSI can be confused by noisy individual scores. Combining all three with different weights makes the composite robust to single-signal failure — a critical property when student lives are on the line.

Worked Composite — Wanjiku

From Slide 8 we had CW(STEM)=56.9. Suppose CosineSim(STEM)=0.78 (scaled to 78) and PSI(STEM)=62.

composite(STEM) = 0.60×56.9 + 0.25×78 + 0.15×62 = 34.14 + 19.50 + 9.30 = 62.94

If composite(SS) = 58.2 and composite(Arts) = 41.0, then STEM ranks first — but the system still must verify the core subject gates before recommending it.

Slide 11 · Gate Checks & Decision Logic

How the Final Pathway is Chosen

Highest composite score does not automatically win. The system enforces hard subject-level gates and falls back through three tiers if no pathway is fully eligible.

Gate 1 · Composite Ranking

Rank all 3 pathways by their composite score. The top one becomes the candidate.

Gate 2 · Core Subject Check

The candidate pathway must pass its hard core gates. A student must score ≥ AE1 (31%) in:

PathwayHard requirementOR
STEMMATH ≥ 31%INT_SCI or SCI_TECH ≥ 31%
Social Sci—ENG or KIS_KSL ≥ 31%
Arts—CRE_ARTS or SPORTS_PE ≥ 31%

STEM Mathematics is non-negotiable. Below 31% in Math = immediate STEM disqualification, regardless of other scores. This is the most consequential rule in the system.

Tiebreaker · Primary Key Subject Average

When the top two pathways are within 5 composite points, the system compares the average of just the primary key subjects:

if abs(comp₁ − comp₂) < 5:
  compare avg(primary_keys₁) vs avg(primary_keys₂)

Switch to the second pathway only if it leads by > 5 points on that comparison. This catches genuine aptitude that the composite missed due to noise in non-key subjects.

3-Tier Fallback Cascade

Tier 1 — Fully Eligible ✓

Composite meets pathway threshold AND core gates pass. Recommend confidently.

↓ if Tier 1 yields no pathway
Tier 2 — Core Passes, Low Score ⚠

Core gates pass but composite below threshold. Recommend with explicit warning: "Consider further preparation."

↓ if Tier 2 also empty
Tier 3 — Best Available with Strong Warning ✗

No pathway passes core. Pick the one requiring the least remediation — smallest gap to core requirements. Issues mandatory teacher review flag.

Why core gates matter more than scores

Imagine a student with 85% averages in everything except 18% in Mathematics. Their composite for STEM might still be high (because the other STEM-relevant subjects are excellent). Without the gate, the algorithm would recommend STEM — and the student would fail Math in Senior Secondary, fail university entry, lose their academic future. The gate stops this. We protect the student, not the algorithm.

Tier 3 in Practice — Rare But Critical

Tier 3 fires for ~2% of students in our test cohort. Every Tier 3 case triggers a mandatory human review before placement. The system never takes irreversible action on a Tier 3 student without teacher sign-off.

Slide 13 · Q-Learning — The Mathematical Core

Q-Learning — Learning the "Value" of Each Action

The central idea of Q-learning: for every (state, action) pair, learn a number called the Q-value = "if I take action a from state s, what is the total reward I expect from now until the end?"

The Q Function

Q(s, a) = expected total future reward
if I take action a from state s

If we know Q for every possible (s, a), then choosing the best action becomes trivial: pick the action with the highest Q.

a* = argmaxa Q(s, a)

The Bellman Equation

How do we compute Q? Through the Bellman equation — the mathematical heart of all RL:

Q(s, a) = r + γ · maxa' Q(s', a')

In English: "The value of doing action a from state s equals the immediate reward r, plus the discounted value of the best action from the next state s'."

γ (gamma) — the discount factor γ ∈ [0, 1] determines how much we care about future rewards vs immediate ones. γ=0 → only immediate. γ=1 → all future rewards equal. We use γ=0.95 — strongly future-oriented but with some discount.

Tiny Q-Table Example

In a simple world with 3 states and 2 actions, Q is a table:

StateAction: LeftAction: Right
S1 (start)2.58.7 ★
S2 (middle)4.19.2 ★
S3 (goal)10.0 ★0.0

The agent reads the row for the current state and picks the column with the highest number. From S1, "Right" has Q=8.7 vs Left=2.5 → pick Right.

The Update Rule

Each time the agent takes an action and sees what happens, it updates Q by moving it slightly toward the Bellman target:

Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α · [target − Q(s,a)]
target = r + γ · maxa' Q(s', a')

α is the learning rate. The difference (target − Q) is called the TD error — the agent's "surprise". Big surprises cause big updates.

Why this works

Even though we never directly know "true Q", iterating the Bellman update with experience makes Q converge to the correct values. This is called Q-iteration and is mathematically proven to converge under mild conditions.

Slide 14 · From Q-Table to Deep Q-Network

Why We Need a Neural Network — The Curse of Dimensionality

Q-tables work fine for tiny problems. For our problem they catastrophically fail. Here's why — and how Deep Q-Networks (DQNs) solve it.

The Counting Problem

Our state has 78 dimensions. Even if each dimension only had 10 possible values:

|states| = 1078

That's more than the number of atoms in the observable universe (~10⁸⁰). We cannot store a Q-table this large. We cannot visit every state to learn its Q.

The Insight We don't actually need to memorise every state. Similar states should have similar Q-values. A student with [Math=70, Sci=68] should get a similar action recommendation as one with [Math=72, Sci=66]. We need a function that generalises — interpolates between known cases. Neural networks are exactly that.

The DQN Idea

Replace the Q-table with a neural network that takes a state as input and outputs Q-values for all 9 actions:

Qθ(s) = [Q(s, a₀), Q(s, a₁), …, Q(s, a₈)]

θ are the network's weights (millions of numbers). We train them so Qθ approximates the true Q as closely as possible.

Training a DQN — The Twist

The standard supervised learning loss won't work directly. We're trying to minimise:

L(θ) = [Qθ(s, a) − target]2
target = r + γ · maxa' Qθ(s', a')

The problem: the target depends on Qθ itself. Updating θ to fit one target moves the target. This is unstable — the network chases its own tail and diverges.

Two Stabilising Tricks (DeepMind 2015)

  1. Target Network — keep a frozen copy of Qθ that we use to compute targets. Update it only every N steps. This breaks the chasing-tail dynamic.
  2. Experience Replay — store past (s, a, r, s') tuples in a buffer. Sample random batches from it to train. This breaks the temporal correlation that would otherwise destabilise training.

Two More Improvements We Use

  1. Double DQN — fixes the "overestimation bias" of vanilla DQN.
  2. Dueling Architecture — splits Q into "state value" + "action advantage" for more efficient learning.

Both explained in the next slide.

Slide 15 · Double Dueling DQN

Double DQN + Dueling Architecture — The Two Refinements We Use

Vanilla DQN has known weaknesses. Double DQN fixes the overestimation bias (van Hasselt 2015). Dueling architecture (Wang 2016) makes learning faster by separating "how good is this state" from "how much does this specific action add". We use both.

Problem 1 · Overestimation Bias

Vanilla DQN's target uses maxa' Q(s', a'). The max operator picks the highest of several noisy estimates — and the highest noisy estimate is, on average, an overestimate. This bias compounds: Q-values inflate over training, the agent becomes overconfident, performance degrades.

Double DQN Solution

Decouple the action selection from the action evaluation. Use the online network to select the best action, but the target network to evaluate its value:

target = r + γ · Qtarget(s', argmaxa' Qonline(s', a'))

Two independent networks won't have correlated noise, so the max no longer systematically overestimates. Simple fix, big improvement.

Problem 2 · Wasted Capacity

In many states, the choice of action barely matters — they all have similar Q. The network spends capacity learning fine differences between actions when it should just be learning "this state is good/bad".

Dueling Architecture Solution

Split the network into two streams that recombine at the end:

  • Value stream V(s) — one number, "how good is this state regardless of action".
  • Advantage stream A(s, a) — vector, "how much better/worse is each action than average from this state".
Q(s, a) = V(s) + A(s, a) − mean(A(s, ·))

The mean subtraction is a normalisation trick that makes the decomposition unique.

Slide 16 · Prioritized Experience Replay (PER)

PER — Sampling From the Replay Buffer Smartly

Vanilla experience replay samples uniformly from the buffer. But not all experiences are equally useful — the ones the network gets most wrong contain the most learning signal. PER samples them more often.

The Core Idea

Each transition (s, a, r, s') gets a priority based on how surprised the network was by it:

priority = (|TD error| + ε)α

α=0.6 (balance between uniform and pure-priority). ε=0.01 (so even zero-error experiences get sampled occasionally). When we update Q, we recompute the new TD error and update the priority.

Importance Sampling Correction

If we sample non-uniformly, our gradient estimate becomes biased. We correct this by weighting the loss for each sample:

wi = (N · P(i))−β, β: 0.4 → 1.0

β starts low (mild correction, fast learning) and anneals to 1 (full correction, asymptotic correctness).

What is "TD error"? It's the difference between the Q-value the network predicted and the Q-value the Bellman equation says it should be. Big TD error = the network was very wrong. PER says: "if you were very wrong, replay this experience more often until you stop being wrong about it."

The SumTree Data Structure

Naively sampling proportional to priority is O(N). For a 20,000-element buffer, that's far too slow. We use a SumTree: a binary tree where each leaf holds a priority and each internal node holds the sum of its children. Sampling becomes O(log N).

    [total: 100]
   /     \
  [60]    [40]
  /  \    /  \
[35][25] [10][30] ← leaves = priorities

Sampling: generate random number r ∈ [0, 100]. Walk down the tree: at each internal node, go left if r ≤ left_sum, else right (and subtract left_sum). Land at the leaf. O(log N).

Action Selection: ε-greedy

During training, balance exploration vs exploitation:

a = random with prob ε
a = argmax Q(s,·) with prob 1−ε

ε starts at 1.0 (totally random) and decays to 0.01 (almost always greedy) with multiplicative decay rate 0.997.

Inferring Actions from Real Data

Real student records don't have an "action taken" label. We infer it: compare G8 vs G9 scores → which subjects improved most? → map to the action that targets those subjects. For STEM students: if MATH improved more → infer A0; if INT_SCI/PRE_TECH improved more → infer A1.

Why discrete actions, not continuous?

Continuous action spaces require actor-critic methods (DDPG, PPO) which are harder to train. Discrete actions let us use vanilla Q-learning. They also map cleanly to teacher-friendly recommendations — "focus on Math" is intuitive in a way "Math weight: 0.62, Eng weight: 0.18, …" is not.

Slide 19 · Reward Function

Reward Function — What the Agent Optimises

The reward signal defines "good" for the DQN. Get the reward wrong and the agent will learn the wrong thing — perfectly. Our reward has 4 components, each measuring a different dimension of "did this recommendation help?"

R = 0.35·ΔSuit + 0.35·ΔStrength + 0.15·Pref + 0.15·Feedback

R₁ · Δ Suitability (weight 0.35)

R₁ = (suitafter − suitbefore) / 100

After the action, did the student's score-derived pathway suitability improve? Suitability is computed from the weighted average of pathway-relevant subject scores. Score-derived (not cluster weight) for a cleaner gradient signal.

R₂ · Δ Strength (weight 0.35)

R₂ = mean(scoreafter − scorebefore)
over targeted subjects only

Did the specific subjects the action targeted actually improve? Action A0 targets MATH — R₂ is positive only if MATH score improved. This gives the DQN fine-grained, action-specific learning signal beyond the broad suitability.

R₃ · Preference Alignment (weight 0.15)

Rewards actions that point toward the student's stated preferred pathway:

  • Action targets preferred pathway: +1.0
  • Action pushes away from preference: −0.2
  • Action A5 (deepen current): +0.3

R₄ · Feedback Agreement (weight 0.15)

Rewards actions that match what the student told us:

Student saysActionR
SatisfiedA5 (deepen)+1.0
SatisfiedStrengthen current+0.5
SatisfiedExplore alt−0.3
Wants diffToward preferred+1.0
Wants diffA6 / A7+0.5
Wants diffOther−0.2

R₅ · Teacher Feedback (planned)

Every teacher override during HITL becomes a strong negative reward; every teacher endorsement, a positive one. Closing this loop will let the system learn from experienced educators in production.

Why split into 4 components instead of 1?

Each component measures a different aspect of "good". A single combined number would hide whether the student improved because the action was correct (R₁/R₂) or because we got lucky. The 4-component design lets us tune weights per dimension and interpret why the agent took a particular action.

Slide 20 · Training Pipeline

How the DQN is Trained — End-to-End

From raw student records to a trained model: extract transitions, augment, fill the buffer, train for 1000 epochs with Double DQN targets, ε-greedy exploration, and prioritized replay sampling.

Phase 1 · Transition Extraction

For each student, for each consecutive grade pair (G7→G8, G8→G9):

  • a
    Build state vector from grades up to G_before
  • b
    Infer the implicit action from the score deltas
  • c
    Compute reward from score changes (4-component)
  • d
    Build next_state from grades up to G_after
  • e
    Store (s, a, r, s', done) tuple

The Terminal G9 Self-Loop

After all real transitions, we add one extra (G9 → G9) self-loop with reward 0. Why? Without it, the network only ever sees G7 or G8 as the "current" state. At inference time, we feed it a G9 state — distribution shift caps accuracy at ~72%. The self-loop trains directly on G9 states. Bumped accuracy to ~78%.

Phase 2 · Augmentation (×3)

Each transition is added 3 times: once original + twice with Gaussian noise σ=0.02 on state vectors. Triples the effective training set without changing the underlying signal.

augmented_state = clip(state + N(0, 0.02), 0, 1)

Phase 3 · Training Loop (1000 epochs)

  • 1
    Sample batch of 128 from PER buffer (high-error samples weighted up)
  • 2
    Forward pass through online network → Q(s, ·)
  • 3
    Compute Double DQN targets via target network
  • 4
    Calculate TD errors per sample
  • 5
    Backprop through dueling streams (V + A − mean A)
  • 6
    Adam optimizer step (lr=5e-4) with gradient clipping to [−1, 1]
  • 7
    Update PER priorities with new TD errors
  • 8
    Every 10 epochs: copy online weights → target weights
  • 9
    Decay ε: ε ← max(ε × 0.997, 0.01)

Human-in-the-Loop — The Teacher Always Wins

No AI system should make irreversible decisions about a child's life without human oversight. Every recommendation passes through a teacher review step. Every override is logged. The AI is an assistant, not an authority.

The HITL Workflow

  • 1
    System computes pathway recommendation + DQN coaching action
  • 2
    Result slip generated with full explanation (why this pathway, why this action)
  • 3
    Teacher reviews. They can: accept, modify, or override with reason
  • 4
    Final decision logged to immutable audit trail
  • 5
    Override events feed back as training data — DQN learns from teacher wisdom
  • 6
    Aggregated override patterns reviewed quarterly for bias and model drift

Mandatory Review Triggers

  • Tier 3 fallback (no pathway passes core)
  • Composite score < 40 across all pathways
  • Stated preference opposite to recommendation
  • Confidence (softmax) < 0.5 for top pathway
  • DQN action A8 (general improvement needed)

Ethical Principles

Transparency

Every recommendation comes with a full breakdown: which signals contributed how much, which gates were checked, which subjects were primary. No black-box decisions.

Reversibility

Recommendations are recommendations, not assignments. Until a teacher signs off, nothing is committed. Even after sign-off, students retain the right to appeal and re-apply.

Auditability

Every input, every signal value, every model version, every teacher action is logged. If a student's family disputes a recommendation, we can reproduce exactly how it was made.

Fairness

The model only sees subject scores and stated preference. No name, gender, school, region, or family background. Fairness audits run quarterly across protected demographic groups.

The "First, Do No Harm" Rule

If the system is uncertain (low confidence, contradictory signals), it abstains and escalates to a human. Better to ask than to misplace.
